# TCA Voice Examples

Calibration document for `tca-content-writer` and `tca-email-campaigns` skills. Use these patterns as the reference for what Thomas Clark sounds like in writing. When in doubt, match the rhythm and stance shown here.

Source material: actual TCA blog posts (April 2026 publication run) plus direct chat patterns from Thomas's own messages to Claude.

---

## Voice in one paragraph

Direct, calm, opinionated. Explains complex things in plain words but doesn't dumb them down. Takes positions — particularly on variable annuities, income-floor planning, and the gap between essential expenses and guaranteed income. Uses concrete metaphors (house with a foundation, bucket strategy, paycheck vs. portfolio). First person is fine and frequently used. Never sounds like a big-firm advisor. Empathetic without being soft.

---

## Sentence-level patterns

### Open with an image, not a thesis statement

> Picture your retirement finances as a house. The floor — the foundation — is income you can count on no matter what happens in the economy, the stock market, or interest rates.

> Some retirees with $2 million saved are anxious about running out of money, while others with $600,000 sleep soundly — the difference often comes down to whether essential expenses are covered by guaranteed income.

The pattern: open with something the reader can see or feel, then turn the corner into the financial point. No "In today's complex financial landscape" type lead-ins. Ever.

### Take a position, then explain why

> This is why I always start planning conversations with income, not assets.

> Variable annuities are not part of this discussion. They combine market risk with high fees and are not how I build an income floor.

The bolded position followed by the reasoning is core to the voice. Don't soften this into "Some advisors prefer" or "It can sometimes make sense to." If Thomas has a view, state it and stand on it.

### "Thomas's Take" callouts

These are formatted as blockquotes inside posts. They are short, opinionated, and synthesize the section's point in one or two sentences:

> **Thomas's Take:** I've found that the single greatest predictor of retirement happiness isn't net worth — it's the gap between essential expenses and guaranteed income. Close that gap, and almost everything else becomes manageable.

> **Thomas's Take:** The income floor doesn't just protect your money — it protects your behavior. When your grocery bill doesn't depend on the S&P 500, you make better investment decisions.

Pattern: contrarian or sharper-than-conventional take, expressed as a personal observation, ending with a practical implication. One per post is plenty. Two if the post is long.

### Hypothetical scenarios with named people

> Consider this hypothetical: Richard and Diane, both 66, have the following income sources...

> Patricia, 64, is planning to retire next year. Here's her situation...

Pattern: real-sounding names (Richard, Diane, Patricia, Margaret, Frank, Susan), specific ages, a labeled "hypothetical" or italicized note that this is illustrative. Then run the actual numbers — Social Security, pension, expenses, gap — in concrete dollars. Vague "a couple in their sixties" never lands. The specifics are what make it feel real.

### Numbers carry the argument

> Their gap is $2,600/month — $31,200/year. From a $500,000 portfolio, that's a 6.2% withdrawal rate just for essentials.

Pattern: lay out the math the reader can follow. Don't ask them to take it on faith. The credibility comes from showing the work.

### Closing synthesis, not a summary list

The end of a TCA post pulls the threads together in prose. It does not say "In summary, here are the five takeaways." It says something like:

> The most resilient retirement plans share one thing: a strong income floor. When your essential expenses are covered by guaranteed, lifetime income, every other financial decision becomes easier — and less stressful.

Pattern: state the synthesizing principle, restate it through what it does for the reader, end clean. No "I hope this helped" — that's not the voice.

## Phrasing that sounds like Thomas

These are Thomas's own constructions, pulled from blog posts and chats. Use them directly when they fit, and use the rhythm of them when building new sentences.

- "Here's where it gets interesting."
- "The math is simple."
- "Most people don't realize…"
- "I've found that…"
- "In my practice…"
- "The right answer depends entirely on your personal situation."
- "This is the power of building an income floor strategically."
- "Close that gap, and almost everything else becomes manageable."
- "It tells you how much additional income you need from savings."
- "Each layer has a specific job, and no layer is asked to do something it's not suited for."
- "The single greatest predictor of [X] isn't [conventional answer] — it's [Thomas's answer]."

From chat patterns specifically (these are how Thomas himself communicates):

- "Yeah" as a sentence opener when agreeing
- "Needs to have certain standards." — short declaratives, often as standalone sentences
- "Just combine it." — instruction without padding
- "Lets pivot to" — direct task switching, no preamble

## Things Thomas does NOT say

These get edited out on every pass:

- "In today's complex financial landscape"
- "Navigating retirement"
- "Empower yourself"
- "Take control of your finances"
- "It's important to remember that"
- "We all know that"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Financial advisors agree"
- "In conclusion"
- Anything that starts with "Are you ready to…"
- Anything that ends with "…and beyond"
- Em-dash overuse where a period works
- "Whether you're [audience A], [audience B], or [audience C], this guide will…"

## Stance on specific topics

This is the voice's editorial position. The skills enforce these as content rules, but they show up in the voice too.

**Variable annuities:** Not part of how Thomas builds retirement income. Stated plainly, not euphemistically.

**Fixed-indexed annuities (FIAs):** Used for income, not growth. Always paired with a guaranteed-lifetime-income rider in TCA content. Never positioned as a market alternative.

**Bucket planning:** Now / Soon / Later. Soon bucket is where the income floor lives. Always tied back to what each layer is "asked to do."

**Social Security claiming:** The single most powerful lever in most retirement plans. Delaying is the default recommendation when there's no health or income reason to claim early — but the post should walk through the actual math, not assert it.

**Tax planning:** Most retirement tax savings happen in November and December, not in April when you file. This framing works.

**Net worth vs. guaranteed income:** Guaranteed income matters more than total net worth for retirement happiness. This is the bedrock TCA position.

## Length and rhythm

- Posts run 1,200–1,800 words.
- Paragraphs are short. Two to four sentences is the norm. A one-sentence paragraph for emphasis is fine and used regularly.
- Sections are 150–300 words. If a section runs longer, break it.
- One table or one comparison element per major section, not three.
- "Key Takeaways" block at top — five bullets, bold lead phrase, then explanation.
- "FAQ" block near the end — three to five questions, each answered in one paragraph.

## Tone calibration by content type

**Blog posts:** Conversational but authoritative. Opinionated where Thomas has a view; explanatory where the goal is to teach.

**Newsletter lead essays:** Slightly more personal than blog posts. Can reference what Thomas is seeing in the market, what came up in client conversations (in general terms), or what's been on his mind. Still 200–400 words.

**Social posts:** Tighter. The strongest single sentence from the blog post, often a "Thomas's Take," reframed for the platform. No hashtag stuffing.

**Email campaigns:** Calm, direct, never hyped. Subject lines are observations or specific claims, not curiosity-bait. "The 76% Social Security increase most people leave on the table" lands. "You won't BELIEVE what's happening with Social Security" does not.
