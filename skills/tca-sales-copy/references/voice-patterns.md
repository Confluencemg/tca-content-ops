# Thomas Clark Voice Patterns — Living Reference

This document captures patterns observed in Thomas's voice from daily conversations, blog content, and corrections made on prior copy. It is intentionally living — update as patterns emerge or change.

## Base register characteristics

**Direct.** Thomas doesn't bury the point. He says what he thinks and gets to the conclusion fast. Sentences tend to be active voice, present tense, declarative. He doesn't pad with throat-clearing.

**Confident without bragging.** Eighteen years of experience shows up as quiet authority, not credentialism. He'll say "I've seen this play out hundreds of times" but won't list designations or AUM as proof. The proof is in the specificity of what he says next.

**Strong opinions plainly stated.** He has positions and he holds them. Variable annuities are counterproductive for retirement income. Bucket planning works. FIAs are for income, not growth. He doesn't hedge into mush. Copy should preserve this — soft-pedaling his opinions makes the voice generic.

**Conversational, not corporate.** Reads like Thomas talking, not like a brochure. Contractions are normal. "It's," "you'll," "we're," "won't." Corporate registers ("In an effort to provide comprehensive solutions...") are wrong.

**Empathetic without saccharine.** Thomas understands the buyer's actual situation — pre-retirees worrying about whether they'll outlive their money, families who haven't talked about what happens when a parent dies. He acknowledges this directly without making it feel like a Hallmark card.

**Educational first.** Default mode is teach, not sell. Even sales copy should educate the reader to the point where buying makes obvious sense. Pressure tactics are off-brand because the voice is fundamentally that of a teacher who happens to have a product.

**Anti-hype.** Thomas finds pushy and overly promising sales copy "cringeworthy" (his word). Voice actively avoids the patterns that make him cringe — exclamation point clusters, "AMAZING!" descriptors, "secret" framing, doomsday hooks, manufactured urgency.

## Sentence-level patterns

**Sentence length variation.** Short, declarative sentences mixed with longer explanatory ones. The rhythm comes from the mix. All-short reads choppy. All-long reads dense.

**Active voice.** "I'll show you" not "You will be shown." "The binder organizes" not "The binder is designed to facilitate organization."

**Specific over general.** "Your spouse needs to know the password to the Fidelity account" beats "Your loved ones need access to your financial information." The specificity is the credibility.

**Examples and analogies.** Thomas explains complex things with concrete examples. Bucket planning is explained through Now/Soon/Later, not through abstract financial theory. Copy should reach for concrete examples over abstract claims.

## Vocabulary patterns

**Words and phrases Thomas uses naturally:**
- "Bucket planning" (his framework — not "tiered approach" or other variations)
- "Now bucket / Soon bucket / Later bucket"
- "Guaranteed income floor"
- "Income-focused FIAs" (when distinguishing from growth annuities)
- "Counterproductive" (re: variable annuities)
- "Fixed Index Annuity" or "FIA" (not "indexed annuity")
- "Pre-retirees" (his demographic)
- "Distribution phase" / "accumulation phase"

**Words and phrases that are NOT Thomas:**
- "Game-changer," "next-level," "rockstar," "crushing it" (info-marketer vocabulary)
- "Take control of your financial future" (generic financial copy)
- "Achieve your dreams," "live your best life" (motivational coaching vocabulary)
- "Secret formula," "hidden truth," "they don't want you to know"
- "Revolutionary," "groundbreaking," "paradigm shift"
- "Folks," "y'all" (regional registers Thomas doesn't use in writing)
- Excessive hedging: "could potentially help you possibly consider..."

## Channel adaptations

**Blog posts** — most conversational, longest sentences allowed, room for digressions and asides. Reads like Thomas thinking out loud.

**Sales pages** — tighter than blog, more structured, but still conversational. The voice flexes toward "Thomas explaining why this product makes sense" rather than "Thomas writing a marketing page."

**Email** — shortest, most direct. Reads like Thomas wrote a personal email, not a marketing blast. First-person singular almost throughout.

**YouTube scripts** — conversational with built-in pacing for delivery. Shorter sentences, more rhetorical questions, more "let me show you what I mean" type transitions.

**Book copy / KDP descriptions** — slightly more formal than blog, appropriate to the medium. Still recognizably Thomas, but in "author voice" rather than "blog voice."

## AI-tells to actively scrub

These are the patterns that read as machine-generated and break voice fidelity:

**Em-dash clusters.** Multiple em-dashes per paragraph signals AI-written prose. Use sparingly and only when the rhythm genuinely calls for one.

**"It's not just X — it's Y" rhythm.** Heavily overused by AI. Avoid unless it's the only natural phrasing.

**Lists of three.** "Direct, confident, and clear." AI defaults to three. Mix it up — sometimes two, sometimes four, sometimes a single strong word.

**Opening with "In today's [world / market / economy]".** Generic AI opener. Thomas would just start with the actual point.

**"Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this" / "At the end of the day".** AI transition phrases that don't sound like Thomas.

**"Whether you're X or Y, this is for you".** Trying to capture multiple audiences at once. Thomas writes to one specific reader at a time.

**Weather-report sentence rhythms.** Every sentence the same length, same structure, same energy. Real prose has variation. Read draft aloud — if it sounds like a metronome, rewrite.

**"Here's the thing" / "Here's what I mean".** AI filler transitions. Either say the thing or don't.

## How to use this document

**Before drafting:** Skim this document to recalibrate voice expectations.

**During drafting:** When uncertain about a phrase, check the AI-tells section and the vocabulary patterns. If it appears in the AI-tells list or contradicts the vocabulary patterns, rewrite.

**After drafting:** Read the draft aloud. If it doesn't sound like Thomas would actually say it, rewrite that section. The aloud-test catches more voice problems than any analytical check.

**After Thomas's redlines:** Update the Voice corrections log with the pattern observed. Over time this section becomes the most useful part of the document.
