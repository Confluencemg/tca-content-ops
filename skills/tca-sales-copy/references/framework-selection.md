# Framework Selection Decision Tree

When writing long-form sales copy, multiple frameworks can apply. Use this decision tree to pick the right one for the right job. Most sales pages use 4-6 frameworks layered together — one for narrative arc, one for offer construction, one for hooks, one for objection handling, etc.

## For overall page narrative arc

**StoryBrand (Donald Miller)** — DEFAULT for most TCA products
Use when: writing the overall structure of a sales page, About page, or any copy where the buyer needs to see themselves as the hero.
Structure: Character (the buyer) has a Problem, meets a Guide (Thomas) who gives them a Plan (the product), calls them to Action, ending in Success or avoiding Failure.
Why it fits: The buyer is the hero, not Thomas. Thomas is the guide because he's been doing this for 18+ years. The product is the plan. This positioning naturally avoids the "look how great I am" trap and naturally aligns with the publishing-not-advisory posture.

**Hero's Journey (compressed)** — for book sales pages and book descriptions
Use when: selling a narrative book, financial fiction, or memoir-style content.
Why: longer-form fiction and narrative non-fiction need a different arc than a digital product page.

## For offer construction and stacking value

**Hormozi's Value Equation** — DEFAULT for any product with a defined offer
Formula: Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort and Sacrifice)
Use when: deciding what to include in the offer, what bonuses to add, how to price, how to address each variable.
Application: If perceived likelihood is low → add proof, guarantees, examples. If time delay is high → add quick-win components or done-for-you elements. If effort is high → add templates, fillable PDFs, video walkthroughs.
Voice note: the FORMULA guides what to include; the LANGUAGE describing it stays in Thomas's calm-confident register, never Hormozi's high-energy delivery.

**Stack Logic (without the hype)** — for products with multiple components
Use when: presenting what's included.
Adapt Brunson's Stack to a calmer presentation: list components clearly with their value, but skip the "$2,997 value!" sticker pricing and the "BUT WAIT" pacing. Present as: "Here's what's in the binder package: [component 1] — [what it does]. [component 2] — [what it does]." Etc.

## For headlines and hooks

**Schwartz's Five Levels of Awareness** — DEFAULT for the hero headline
Levels: Unaware → Problem-Aware → Solution-Aware → Product-Aware → Most-Aware
Use when: writing the hero headline and section headlines.
Mapping for TCA products:
- **Just in Case Binder buyer**: mostly problem-aware (they know they need to organize this, don't know how)
- **Community buyer**: solution-aware (they know they need education, evaluating options)
- **Book buyer**: depends on the book — narrative non-fiction often unaware/problem-aware, how-to books usually solution-aware
- **Course buyer**: typically solution-aware to product-aware

Wrong-level diagnosis: if the headline talks about features (most-aware language) to a problem-aware reader, conversion dies. If the headline talks about big picture problems (problem-aware) to a most-aware reader who's already shopping, they bounce because it's too basic.

**Epiphany Bridge (Brunson)** — for story-led openings
Use when: opening with Thomas's own moment of realization, or when the page benefits from origin-story framing.
Structure: Backstory → Wall (the problem) → Epiphany (the realization) → Plan (what changed) → Result (what happened) → Transformation (the new identity).
Voice adaptation: Brunson's version is energetic and exclamation-heavy. Thomas's version is calmer, more reflective. Same structure, different register.

## For section openings and short copy

**PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)** — workhorse for short sections
Use when: writing any section under 500 words that needs to convert.
Structure: Name the problem clearly. Make the reader feel the cost of not solving it. Present the solution.
Voice note: "Agitate" doesn't mean "manipulate." For Thomas, agitation is just being honest about consequences. "If your spouse can't find the password to the Fidelity account, the kids will spend three months on the phone with their estate attorney" — that's agitation through honesty, not fear-mongering.

**AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)** — for medium sections (500-1500 words)
Use when: a section is too long for PAS but doesn't need full StoryBrand treatment.
Structure: Hook for attention, develop interest with specifics, build desire with outcomes, close with action.

**BAB (Before-After-Bridge)** — for transformation-focused sections
Use when: showing what life looks like before the product, after the product, and how the product is the bridge.
Especially useful for: the "What's included" section where you want to show what each component changes.

## For FAQ and objection handling

**Brunson's Three False Belief Patterns** — DEFAULT for FAQ sections
Categories:
1. **Vehicle objections**: "Does this approach actually work?" — they doubt the method
2. **Internal objections**: "Can I actually do this?" — they doubt themselves
3. **External objections**: "Will my circumstances allow it?" — they doubt the world

Every real objection collapses into one of these three. For the binder, examples:
- Vehicle: "Will my family actually use a binder?"
- Internal: "I'm not organized enough to maintain this"
- External: "My situation is too complicated for a template"

Address all three categories explicitly in the FAQ. Missing one = leaving conversion on the table.

## For the close and CTA

**Single-action close** — DEFAULT
One CTA, one button, one outcome. No three-tier "starter/pro/premium" choice paralysis on the binder.

**Risk reversal language** — when guarantees are offered
"30-day refund if it doesn't deliver what we said" — direct, simple, no carnival framing.

## Layering example

For the Just in Case Binder Gumroad page, the layered framework selection looks like:
- **Overall arc**: StoryBrand (family is hero, Thomas is guide, binder is plan)
- **Hero headline**: Schwartz problem-aware level
- **Hero section opening**: Epiphany Bridge (compressed) — Thomas's story of why this exists
- **Problem section**: PAS
- **What's included**: BAB applied to each component
- **Value framing**: Hormozi value equation in the background, never explicit
- **FAQ**: Brunson's three false-belief categories
- **Close**: Single-action close with risk reversal

That's six frameworks in one page, each doing one job, none of them visible to the reader as "frameworks." The reader just sees a page that makes sense and feels coherent.

## When to override defaults

Override when:
- Thomas explicitly requests a different framework
- The product is structurally different (e.g., a $497 course vs a $27 binder needs different treatment)
- The audience is unusual (e.g., copy aimed at advisors instead of consumers)
- A test has shown a specific framework underperforms for this product

Document the override in the framework notes section of the delivery so we learn what works.
